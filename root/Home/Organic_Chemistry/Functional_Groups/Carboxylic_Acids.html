Carboxylic Acids

Created Friday 21 January 2022
@MISC @ORGCHEM

@Carboxylic_acid

Carboxyllic acids can be obtained by @Oxidation of alcohols or @Aldehyde

Examples:
@Acetic_acid
Long chain acids from fat breakdown
@Perfluorinated_alkylated_substances

Structure

Carbon has SP2 @Hybridisation
High boiling point because of strong hydrogen bonding

Dissociation

They are acids → they can donate protons to bases → @Carboxylic_salts

Acidity

Carboxyclic acids have a pK of about 5. More acidic than phenols because of @Resonance

Substituents can affect the acidity too:
Substituents that accept electrons → @carbanion is stabilised → stronger acids
Electron donating substituents → anion is destabilised → more basic
More atoms with @electronegativity → stronger acid

There are @activating_groups and @deactivating_groups
Deaectivating groups lower the pK value ≠ activating groups which raise it.

Derivatives of Carboxylic Acids

@Nucleophilic_acyl_substitution is a general reaction that applies to all @Carboxylic_acid derivatives

The @Nucleophile has substituted itself onto the acyl group and turned the Y into a @leaving_group

The difference in reactivity between CADs and other @functional_group is the leaving group.


Reactivity of Carboxylic Acid Derivatives (CADs)


A @Nucleophile reacts better with unhindered carbonyl groups ( @Steric_hinderance )
@electrophile carbonyl groups are more reactive to addition
The @transition_state intermediate with the best leaving group decomposes first.

@carbonyl_group⬇️

@Amide
@Ester
@Thioester
@Acid_anhydride
@Acid_chloride


You can turn an acid chloride into an amide but you cannot turn an amide back into an acid chloride.


Fischer Esterification

@Fischer_Esterification produces an @Ester from a carboxylic acid, boiled in alcohol and a strong acid. The reaction mechanism is the same as the @Nucleophilic_acyl_substitution above.

Hydrolysis

@Ester and other derivatives can be turned back into carboxylic acid through @hydrolysis in acidic or basic conditions.
In acidic confitions, water is added to the ester to produce an alcohol. This is exactly the opposite of @Fischer_Esterification

In basic conditions, NaOH is added along with water.
