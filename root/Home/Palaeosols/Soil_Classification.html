Why do soils change vertically?

  1. Vegetation on top, dropping leaves, and then decay when they die. Lots of organic matter accumulates.
  2. Living organic matter in the root zone underground. Topsoil is very rich in organic matter. Quickly destroyed by microbial activity.
  3. Amino acids left which decay through @microbial_oxidisation. All organic matter decays into @organic_acids and then carbon dioxide.
  4. Rainwater percolating through the organic rich top soil becomes more acidic and makes @carbonic_acid when it reacts with the carbon dioxide.
  5. Rock fragments in soil will be dissolved in the acidic rain water.
  6. K Na Ca and Mg all get dissolved in the rainwater solution.
  7. The soluble ions act as a buffer solution and slow down the acidification of the water.
  8. The minerals can then ppt out in the B horizon
Calcium ions are very soluble and it has been attempted to estimate the rainfall by looking for the horizon with calcium,but you need to know where the original soil surface was.

Horizons

@Soil_horizon

@Elluvial ≠ @Alluvial horizon is poor in mobile soluble @cations than the underlying stata
B: Minerals start to ppt out of solution. Rich in @Sesquioxides (have 3 oxygens and 2 other atoms like Al2O3 or Fe2O3.)
The @Illuvial horizon ≠ Elluvial is the B horizon enriched in some substance.

Cg: @Gley soils are green or blue hydromorphic (waterlogged) soils where @Gleization has happened. When groundwater stagnates, the small amount of oxygen in the water is consumed by the areobic bactiria. This changes the redox conditions which changes the colour. Often results in a mottled colour.


Not all soils have all the horizons. If there is no vegetation, there won't be an O horizon.

@Regolith is the all the way from the bedrock to the modern @soil surface
@Saprolith is from the ground level to the C horizon

When the perculating water eventually reaches the ground water table it starts to mix with the ground water.
